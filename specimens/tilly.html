Life in the Ancient Seas

Long before dinosaurs, before trees, before almost anything we'd recognize as life on land — trilobites ruled the oceans. For nearly 300 million years, they crawled across ancient seafloors, some as small as a grain of rice, others stretching over two feet long.

They were among the first animals to develop complex compound eyes, made of calcite crystals — essentially lenses carved from stone, hundreds of millions of years before humans ground their first lens. Some species had eyes with over 15,000 individual lenses.

Illustration of trilobites on the Ordovician seafloor. Source: Wikimedia Commons, public domain.

Trilobites were arthropods — relatives of modern crabs, spiders, and insects. They could roll into balls for protection, like modern pill bugs. They molted their exoskeletons as they grew, which is why trilobite fossils are so common — each animal left behind many shells in a lifetime.

🦣 Deep time moment: Trilobites went extinct about 252 million years ago during the Great Dying — the largest mass extinction in Earth's history, which wiped out 96% of marine species. Tilly's ancestors survived four other mass extinctions before that. She comes from tough stock.

How She Joined the Collection

March 19, 2022. The Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History gift shop. A small white box with a handwritten label. Twelve dollars. She fit in the palm of my hand — half a billion years of existence reduced to something I could carry home on the Metro.

I didn't know then that she'd be the beginning of anything. She sat on my desk for years, quietly. It wasn't until 2026 that she got company — and suddenly she wasn't alone anymore. She was the founding specimen of something real.

She's still the smallest thing in the collection. She still has her original handwritten box. She doesn't need a printed card — she predates the whole system. She's grandfathered in.
